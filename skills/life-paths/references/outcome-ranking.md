# Stage 4: Outcome ranking

Goal: extract what the person actually values by showing them concrete futures and watching what they do, instead of asking abstract preference questions. "Do you value autonomy or impact?" is unanswerable and produces socially rehearsed answers. "Here are two ordinary Tuesdays in 2040; which one do you want?" is answerable, and the reasons attach to specifics you can reuse.

## Build the vignettes

Write eight to twelve short scenes, each a plausible future for this specific person, drawn from their dossier: their field, their household, their constraints. Second person, present tense, 60-120 words, one ordinary day or one ordinary year, no path labels attached. A vignette that could belong to anyone has failed; use their materials (the craft they practice, the city question they are weighing, the person they share a life with) so the reaction is to a recognizable life, not a stock photo.

The set must span the axes that later distinguish paths. Cover at least these six, two poles each, deliberately spread across the vignettes rather than one axis per vignette:

- Inside institutions versus independent.
- Publicly visible work versus impact through a position, mostly invisible.
- One deep occupation versus serial variety.
- Optimized for wealth versus optimized for time.
- Recognition versus anonymity.
- Ambition versus rest (include at least one genuinely quiet vignette: modest, local, unimpressive on paper. Its ranking is informative in both directions, and omitting it builds ambition into the instrument).

Include one or two vignettes the dossier suggests they would never pick. Confirmation is cheap; surprise is what this stage is for.

## Run the ranking

Present the vignettes and have them rank all of them, best to worst (use a ranking UI if the environment has one; otherwise a numbered list they reorder). Then, and this is where the value lives:

- For the top two or three: "what specifically in this scene attracts you?" Push past the vignette to the feature: is it the autonomy, the craft, the quiet, the particular kind of colleague?
- For the bottom two or three: same question inverted. Repulsions are usually more diagnostic and more stable than attractions.
- Note hesitations and re-orderings, which mark live tensions.

Then run three or four forced pairs that isolate single axes the ranking left ambiguous: two scenes identical except for the one variable. "Same work, same money; in A it is public under your name, in B anonymous inside an institution" settles the visibility axis in a way no direct question can.

## Cross-check and record

Compare the results against the revealed-preference record from stage 3. Agreement raises confidence. Disagreement gets one gentle pass in the moment ("you ranked the independent scenes on top, and the record shows you have never once worked outside an institution; what do you make of that?") and then gets logged either way; do not force a resolution, and remember that for a person with unreliable introspection, rankings of concrete scenes are usually better evidence than their abstract statements but still weaker than their behavior.

Write `values.md`: each axis with the person's position, a confidence level (high only when ranking and record agree), the specific features they named as attracting and repelling, and any tensions carried forward as open. The path agents read this file to know what each path should optimize for; write it so a fresh reader could act on it.
