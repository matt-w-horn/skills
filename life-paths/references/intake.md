# Stage 1: Intake

Goal: a dossier that lets the later stages reason about this specific person rather than a demographic. The dossier has two halves that must both be filled: the record (what actually happened) and the self-account (what they say about it). Later stages compare the two, so collect them as distinct things.

## Open the process

Explain, briefly and in plain words, what is about to happen: an interview and document-gathering phase, a financial pass, a values exercise, then a set of analyses that produce a few genuinely different long-term paths with a recommendation. Give the honest time cost of the full run and offer light mode. Ask where they want the workspace directory. Then start.

## Artifacts before questions

Ask for documents first; a good artifact answers thirty questions at once and is more reliable than memory. Request whatever exists of:

- Resume or CV, LinkedIn profile, personal site, portfolio.
- Performance reviews, promotion packets, peer feedback, self-reviews. Ask for the critical ones, not just the good ones, and explain why: the friction record is where environment-fit information lives, and it is the material most processes politely ignore. Reviews they disagree with are especially valuable; collect the review and the disagreement.
- Anything they have written or built that they consider representative: essays, talks, side projects, repositories.
- Financial documents get requested here too but handled in stage 2: net-worth statement, income, a spending record (actuals, ideally exportable), any retirement or FIRE model they have built.

Read what they provide before asking questions that the documents already answer; re-asking signals you did not read. Log extracted facts into `evidence.md` with `[artifact: <name>]` tags as you read.

## The interview

Cover six territories. Adapt order and depth to the person; the territories are the checklist, not the script. Ask in batches of one to three questions and wait. When an answer is interesting, follow it before returning to the checklist; the detours are usually the good part.

**1. Ground facts.** Age, household (partner, kids, dependents, pets), location and how attached they are to it, citizenship/visa situation if it could bind, health facts they consider planning-relevant. Country matters for stage 2; get it now.

**2. The career walk-through.** For each significant role, in order: what they actually did, what shipped, why they left, and, the two questions that matter most, what they were repeatedly praised for and what feedback or friction kept recurring. Cross-check against artifacts. You are looking for the repeating unit of their working life: the shape of work they return to across different employers, which is usually more stable and more informative than any job title. Name the pattern back to them once you see it and let them correct it.

**3. How they work.** What they do unprompted: weekends, side projects, the things nobody assigned. What they finish versus abandon, and whether they can say why. Energy: what kind of week leaves them full versus empty. Attention: do they hold one thing for months, or run deep-but-serial? Do not assume either is better; you are calibrating containers, since a path built on ten-year single projects fails a serial-depth person and vice versa.

**4. Constraints.** Money (headline only; stage 2 goes deep), caregiving present or foreseeable, health, partner's career and its geography, anything they have promised someone. Distinguish hard constraints from preferences by asking directly: "if a path required breaking this, is it dead, or just worse?"

**5. Stated desires and fears.** What futures attract them, what futures they dread, what they would do with a free decade, whose career they envy and which part specifically. Record these faithfully as `[stated]`. Do not challenge them here even when they contradict the record; contradictions are stage 3 material, and interrogating a stated desire at the moment it is voiced teaches the person to stop volunteering them.

**6. Self-knowledge calibration.** Ask, in some natural form: how reliable is your own read on what you enjoy? Some people will tell you plainly that they cannot tell, or that they discover what they liked only afterward, or that a partner reads their energy better than they do. Take the answer seriously and record it, because it sets the evidence weights for the whole process: for a person with unreliable introspection, `[behavior]` and `[observer]` evidence outranks `[stated]`, the outcome-ranking stage leans on reactions to concrete scenes rather than abstract preference questions, and the final document's decision gates must use behavioral tests. For a person with reliable introspection, stated preferences carry more weight. Either answer is fine; what fails is assuming.

If they mention a trusted observer (partner, old friend, longtime colleague), offer to include one or two questions relayed through the person: "what work makes them come alive, from the outside?" Optional, often gold.

## Writing the dossier

Maintain `dossier.md` continuously, organized by the six territories plus a "patterns" section for cross-cutting observations, each pattern citing at least two evidence entries. Reflect the emerging picture back to the person at natural intervals, a short paragraph, not a report, so errors die early. The stage ends when the six territories are covered to the depth the chosen mode calls for and the person says the dossier summary reads true.
